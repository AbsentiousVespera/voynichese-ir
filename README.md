# Voynichese-IR

**Reverse-engineering the Voynich Manuscript as a formal language ‚Äî a post-mortem.**

No translation. No cipher-breaking. No plant identification. The manuscript was treated exactly like an
undocumented programming language whose compiler has been lost: normalize the corpus, recover the
structure, induce the smallest grammar that generates it, build a parser, validate everything, ship a spec.

This document is the complete post-mortem of that project: what was built, what was found, what broke,
and what we'd tell the next engineer who picks it up.

---

## TL;DR

The text of Beinecke MS 408 is generated by a **small regular grammar**. Nearly all structure lives
*inside* the word; word order carries almost no information. A 267-edge finite automaton recognizes
96.9% of the manuscript's 37,844 tokens ‚Äî and when you sample random walks from that automaton,
**75.3% of the strings you generate are real, attested Voynich words**. The lexicon nearly *is* the
language. Whatever process produced this text, it followed mechanical, layout-aware rules with
near-total consistency across 227 pages, multiple scribal hands, and both Currier "languages."

Two working tools came out of it:

- **The specification** ‚Äî [`reports/SPEC.md`](reports/SPEC.md), all 15 deliverables
  (rendered page: [`reports/voynichese-ir-spec.html`](reports/voynichese-ir-spec.html))
- **The linter** ‚Äî a live syntax checker that tells you whether any EVA string is processable
  Voynichese, with compiler-grade diagnostics
  (open [`reports/voynichese-linter.html`](reports/voynichese-linter.html) in a browser, or CLI: `npm run lint -- "qokeedy shol daiin"`)

## 1. Context and ground rules

The project began with a single file: the 127 MB Internet Archive PDF of the manuscript scans.
The brief imposed a strict methodology ‚Äî syntax first, semantics last, in this exact order:

```
Document ‚Üí Section ‚Üí Entry ‚Üí Paragraph ‚Üí Line ‚Üí Word Shape ‚Üí Word Family
        ‚Üí Glyph Family ‚Üí Glyph ‚Üí Grammar ‚Üí Semantics (only if justified)
```

Forbidden moves: translating glyphs, identifying plants or zodiac signs, assuming a natural language,
assuming a cipher, comparing against existing Voynich theories. Every rule had to be statistically
justified against the whole corpus, with generality favored over cleverness.

## 2. The first engineering decision (and the most important one)

**You cannot compute on a PDF of vellum.** The scans have no text layer, and building a computer-vision
glyph extractor is a research project of its own. The pragmatic move was to adopt an existing
*transliteration* ‚Äî the *Zandbergen‚ÄìLandini* file (**ZL3b**, IVTFF "Eva-" 2.0 format, 2025-05-13, from
voynich.nu) ‚Äî and treat its ASCII codes as **opaque symbol IDs**, never as letters.

This matters for interpreting every result below: "EVA `q`" means *the glyph shape that the ZL
transliteration writes as q*, nothing more. The transliteration embeds one human judgment we inherit ‚Äî
where one glyph ends and the next begins. That is the project's largest unavoidable bias, and it is
front-loaded here honestly.

The IVTFF format turned out to be a gift: page headers carry metadata variables (`$Q` quire, `$H` hand,
`$L` Currier language, `$I` illustration type), every line is a typed locus (paragraph text vs. label vs.
circular text), and paragraph boundaries are explicitly marked. The lexer's input format was, in effect,
already documented.

## 3. What was built

Node.js, zero dependencies. (The machine had no working Python ‚Äî only the Microsoft Store stub ‚Äî which
turned out to be no loss at all.)

## 4. Findings, in the order they fell out

### Phase 0 ‚Äî Corpus normalization
227 pages, 750 paragraphs, 5,059 lines, 37,844 tokens; 89.4% fully clean (no uncertain glyphs or
alternate readings). Uncertain tokens are kept in the IR but excluded from statistics.

### Phase 1 ‚Äî The document has a recoverable AST (before reading a single glyph)
Eight sections self-identify by illustration type and have *different layout grammars*: herbal pages are
1‚Äì2 short blocks plus labels; stars/recipes pages are dense lists of ~12 blocks; the zodiac section
contains **zero paragraphs** ‚Äî only labels and circular text. Labels are one word 91% of the time.
Paragraph-final lines run short (mean 6.0 tokens vs. 8.7‚Äì9.1) ‚Äî paragraphs are genuine units with fill
behavior, not arbitrary line wrapping.

### Phase 2 ‚Äî Layout is syntax (glyph identities still masked)
- Paragraph-initial words contain an ascender glyph **91.7%** of the time vs. a 51% baseline. The
  language marks *block start* ‚Äî a BEGIN keyword at the layout level.
- Line-final words end in a descender **56.7%** vs. 41.8% elsewhere ‚Äî an END-of-line bias.
- Line-initial words are longer and ascender-poor; adjacent words weakly cluster by shape class.

### Phase 3 ‚Äî The lexicon is statistically weird
- 7,650 types; hapax rate 70.5%; Zipf slope **‚àí1.037** ‚Äî almost perfectly Zipfian.
- Words have positional syntax: `chdy` is never line-initial (0/132) but is enriched line-finally;
  `sho` opens lines at 2.4√ó baseline.
- The Currier A/B split is near-absolute for part of the vocabulary: `qokeedy` (n=303) occurs **0%**
  in A-pages; `chedy` (n=487) 0.2%; meanwhile `chor` is 86% A against 32% expected. Two registers.
- Exact adjacent repetition (`daiin daiin`, `chol chol`) occurs **274 times** ‚Äî far beyond anything
  natural prose does.
- **The single strongest lexical finding:** of the 261 word types with frequency ‚â• 20, **249 form one
  connected component under edit-distance 1**. The lexicon is a single dense combinatorial
  neighborhood. Natural-language vocabularies decompose into many small islands; this looks like an
  enumerated identifier space.

### Phase 4 ‚Äî Glyphs are locked into units
Measured constraints (not assumptions): `q` word-initial 99.0% and followed by `o` 97.7%; `n` word-final
97.2%; `m` final 94.4%; `y` final 86.1%; `h` **never** word-initial; `c‚Üíh` fires at 82.7%; `ii‚Üín` at
95.0%. Successor entropies near zero for `q`, `n`, `m` prove these are parts of multigraph units, not
independent symbols. This justified a unit alphabet: benches `ch`/`sh`, platform-gallows ligatures
`ckh cth cph cfh`, run-length units `e/ee/eee`, `i/ii/iii`, the clitic `q`, and simple glyphs.

The inheritance graph, from statistics alone:

```
GALLOWS  {k t p f} ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚î¨‚îÄ‚îÄ PLATFORMED {ckh cth cph cfh}  (bench ‚àò gallows ligature)
BENCH    ch ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚î¥‚îÄ‚îÄ PLUMED sh                     (modifier variant)

CLITIC   q             (word-initial only, binds o)

```

### Phase 5 ‚Äî Words have slot morphology
Every unit has a *home position*. Mean normalized position inside the word is monotone and tight:
`q` .004 ‚Üí `sh` .138 ‚Üí `ch` .214 ‚Üí gallows ~.3 ‚Üí `a`/`e` ~.47 ‚Üí `d` .50 ‚Üí `ee` .55 ‚Üí `ii` .70 ‚Üí
`l` .69 ‚Üí `r` .85 ‚Üí `y` .88 ‚Üí `m` .97 ‚Üí `n` .99. Words are walks through a fixed template:

```
Word ::= Q? PRE{0..2} CORE{0..5} FIN{0..4}
```

Coverage: **96.65% of all tokens**, 85.5% of types, zero failing types with frequency ‚â• 20.

### Phase 6 ‚Äî The language is Regular (MDL says so)
Minimum-description-length comparison over unit models, model cost included:

| model | bits/token |
|---|---|
| unigram | 21.05 |
| **bigram automaton (regular)** | **12.86** |
| trigram | 13.88 |

Order-1 wins; longer context does not pay for itself. The word language is regular ‚Äî in fact sub-regular
(star-free, slot-ordered), trivially LL(1)/LR(0), PEG-compatible, deterministic as a DFA.

Above the word: adjacent word-class mutual information is **0.110 bits** vs. 0.014 shuffled ‚Äî real but an
order of magnitude weaker than word-internal structure. The one crisp supra-word rule found:
**P(next word begins `q` | current word ends `y`) = 0.281** vs. 0.07‚Äì0.11 after anything else.
No evidence of constituency or phrase structure anywhere.

### Phase 7 ‚Äî The parser, and the bombshell
The pruned DFA (267 edges, count ‚â• 15) recognizes 96.9% of tokens. FIRST set: `o` .225, `ch` .159,
`q` .143, `d` .093, `sh` .084. LAST set: `y` .404, `n` .159, `l` .155, `r` .153.

Then the generative test: sample 2,000 random walks from the automaton. **75.3% are attested word
types.** A recognizer that is simultaneously a near-exhaustive generator means the lexicon ‚âà the
language: the "vocabulary" is close to *all short legal strings* of a small grammar. Sample a bigram
model of English orthography and you get mostly garbage; here you get mostly real Voynich words.

### Phase 8 ‚Äî AST
```
Document ‚Üí Section ‚Üí Entry(page: quire, hand, register) ‚Üí
  Block* ‚Üí Line+ (is-terminal) ‚Üí Word+ (units[], slots{Q,PRE,CORE,FIN})
  LabelSet* ‚Üí Word
```
A full parse of folio 1r is emitted at `build/ast_f1r.json`.

### Phase 9 ‚Äî Validation
- Automaton trained *only* on the herbal section covers 92‚Äì96% of every other section.
- A-register-trained grammar covers **92.3%** of B tokens; B-trained covers **94.2%** of A.
  One morphology governs the entire manuscript across sections, hands, and registers.
- 169 productions with count ‚â• 50 scored by frequency √ó cross-section stability; every top-20 rule
  sits at confidence ‚â• 0.93 (`reports/productions.json`).

### Ambiguity report
Unit segmentation is deterministic under longest-match (`h` is never initial, so ligatures can't
conflict). The DFA is conflict-free. The slot grammar *written as a CFG* is massively ambiguous
(6,465 types admit multiple slot assignments) because slot alphabets overlap ‚Äî the *language* is
unambiguous; that *presentation* is not. Canonical form: the DFA, with slots as a leftmost-longest view.

## 5. The linter

Because the induced grammar is a DFA, a validator was cheap to build and is the most fun deliverable:
paste anything in EVA letters and get a **PROCESSABLE / NOT PROCESSABLE** verdict with per-word
diagnostics ‚Äî exact failure position, legal continuations with probabilities, attested-vs-novel status,
surprisal score, and did-you-mean suggestions from the 2,398-type attested lexicon.

```
$ node src/lint.js "qokeedy hol daiinq chq okeody"
OK     qokeedy   attested 303√ó in the manuscript  [q o k ee d y]
ERROR  hol       'h' only exists inside compound glyphs ‚Äî never stands alone
       did you mean: ol(551x), chol(378x), shol(175x)
ERROR  daiinq    'q' cannot follow 'n' (position 5)
       expected here: (end)(0.973) o(0.008) y(0.005)
       did you mean: daiin(793x), daiiny(3x), aiin(495x)
ERROR  chq       'q' cannot follow 'ch' (position 2)
       expected here: e(0.394) o(0.24) y(0.09)
OK     okeody    attested 34√ó in the manuscript

5 words, 3 errors ‚Äî NOT processable
```

The linter enforces rules nobody ever wrote down. Whoever produced the manuscript obeyed them, page
after page, for ~38,000 words.

## 6. What broke along the way (the actual post-mortem part)

1. **No Python on the machine.** Windows ships a Store-stub `python.exe` that just opens the Microsoft
   Store. Node.js was present and handled everything ‚Äî statistics, MDL, automata, Levenshtein ‚Äî with
   zero dependencies. Lesson: check the runtime before designing the pipeline, and don't assume you
   need the "data science" stack for corpus statistics.
2. **The `t.w` vs `t.t` bug.** The IR stores a token's surface as `.t`; a late script (`export_grammar.js`)
   read `.w`, silently producing `cnt = { undefined: 37844 }` ‚Äî which surfaced as "edges: 0, lexicon
   entries: 1". Diagnosed in one step because the export printed summary stats. Lesson: every pipeline
   stage should print its own sanity numbers; silent `undefined` keys in JS objects are a classic.
3. **The `checkhy` family broke the first slot grammar.** The initial template forbade platform gallows
   after the `e`-nucleus; `checkhy`/`sheckhy`/`checthy` (110+ tokens) failed to parse. The fix ‚Äî allowing
   gallows units in the CORE slot ‚Äî took coverage from 94.9% to 96.65% with zero frequent failures left.
   Lesson: the grammar's failure list *is* the to-do list; the corpus corrects the theorist.
4. **Windows path friction.** Git Bash `require('/d/V/...')` doesn't resolve in Node on Windows ‚Äî use
   `D:/V/...`. PowerShell 5.1 lacks `&&`. Minor, constant, worth knowing.
5. **A CFG presentation of a regular language is a trap.** Writing the slot grammar as a CFG produced
   thousands of spurious ambiguities that say nothing about the language. The DFA is the honest
   canonical form; the slots are a human-readable *view*. Lesson straight from compiler class:
   choose the weakest formalism that fits.

## 7. Threats to validity

- **Transliteration dependence.** All results are relative to ZL3b's glyph segmentation. EVA is designed
  to be analysis-neutral, and the ZL file is the field's most careful transliteration, but "what is one
  glyph" is a human judgment beneath every statistic here. A different granularity (e.g., treating `qo`
  or `dy` as atomic) would change unit-level numbers ‚Äî though not the monotone-slot phenomenon itself,
  which is robust to re-segmentation.
- **Threshold choices.** DFA pruning at edge-count ‚â• 15 and the "rare" band at < 50 are judgment calls;
  coverage/precision trade off smoothly around them. MDL model costs use a simple 24-bits-per-parameter
  encoding; the *ordering* of models (bigram < trigram < unigram) is insensitive to reasonable variations.
- **Small sections undertrain.** Pharmaceutical (2.6k tokens) and text-only (2.4k) sections make weak
  *training* sets in cross-validation; as *test* sets they are covered at 0.91+. The asymmetry is a
  data-size artifact, not a structural one.
- **Labels vs. paragraph text.** Labels were included in lexical statistics but excluded from
  word-order statistics. Their internal morphology is indistinguishable from running text.

## 8. What this constrains (stated carefully)

No semantics were attempted, per the brief. But the structural profile is now a hard test that any
future theory of the manuscript must pass. It must reproduce:

- (a) regular slot morphology inside words,
- (b) lexicon ‚âà language density (75% generative precision, one edit-1 lexical component),
- (c) near-zero word-order information, with the q|y line-coupling,
- (d) layout-level BEGIN/END marking (ascender rule, terminal-line shortening),
- (e) the A/B register split under one shared grammar,
- (f) Zipf slope ‚âà ‚àí1 with 70% hapax, and 274 exact adjacent repetitions.

That profile is statistically unlike natural-language lexica, and unlike a simple substitution of a
natural language (both preserve lexical sparsity and word-order information this corpus lacks). It is
what mechanically-generated text under a tight template looks like. Which mechanism, and whether
anything is encoded in the residual choices, is the next project ‚Äî with semantics earning its place
only after this grammar.

## 9. What we'd do next

1. **Re-segmentation robustness:** re-run the pipeline under alternative unit alphabets (Cuva, v101)
   and confirm the slot monotonicity survives.
2. **Vision phase:** align the IR to the scans and measure glyph geometry directly (the shape grammar
   deserves pixel-level ground truth, not transliteration-mediated classes).
3. **Residual-information analysis:** given the grammar, measure how many bits per word remain free ‚Äî
   the channel capacity of the "choices" the writer actually had ‚Äî and whether those choices correlate
   with anything (position, section, illustration).
4. **A generator:** the flip side of the linter ‚Äî sample statistically faithful pseudo-folios and test
   whether experts can distinguish them from real pages.
